NCAA Revenue Share — How It Works

Incoming $
NCAA & Conference Distributions
The NCAA earns ~$1.1B/year from its March Madness TV deal (CBS/Turner). That money is distributed to conferences based on tournament units — every game your conference participates in earns a unit worth ~$340K (paid over 6 years). Your conference then distributes this + its own media deal money to member schools. More tournament appearances and wins = more units = more money flowing to your school.
Your 2025 total: $1.69M (NCAA) + $1.81M (Conference) = $3.50M
Expense $
House Settlement — Direct Athlete Revenue Share
The 2024 House v. NCAA antitrust settlement lets schools pay athletes directly from a revenue-sharing pool. The cap starts at ~$20.5M/school/year (growing ~4%/yr through the settlement period). This is separate from NIL deals — it's a direct payment from the school. You're currently spending $4.97M, which is only 24% of your allowable cap. Schools spending closer to the cap have a major recruiting advantage.
2025 spend vs cap: $4.97M of $20.5M (24.2%)
The Strategy
Spending More to Earn More
Increasing rev share spend attracts and retains better athletes → better win rates → more ticket sales, bigger donor base, more bowl appearances → more NCAA/conference distribution units. The question your boss needs to answer: how much additional revenue does each extra dollar of rev share generate? That's the "Performance Return" slider in the scenario planner below.
Headroom to invest: ~$15.5M/yr above current spend (2025-26 cap)
